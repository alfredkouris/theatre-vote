A Note from the Chefs

Tonight, we will attempt to bake a cake live on stage.

Not a metaphorical cake. Not a symbolic cake. A real cake. With ingredients and measurements and timing and consequences. A cake that can collapse. A cake that can burn. A cake that, like theatre itself, exists only for us in this room tonight.

When we first began making this show, we talked about the liveness of theatre. Its immediacy. Its danger. Harry had just seen a show in rural Vermont with a printer on stage - printing. Printing images onto paper that became essential props for the actors. Early on, it jammed. For real. And the cast had to fix it in front of the audience's eyes. Dangerous. Real. Live. We wanted to find our version of this. What could we do that had very real consequences and could very easily go wrong...

And then we thought about cake.

And cake, for reasons we still don't fully understand, seems to matter enormously to people.

Maybe it's because cakes are inherently optimistic. Nobody bakes a cake for a funeral. Cakes arrive at birthdays, weddings, office farewells, apologies, reconciliations. They are edible acts of hope. Even a badly baked cake means someone tried.

This show was built the same way many cakes are. Messily. Experimentally. Sometimes with too much heat. Sometimes not enough. There were moments we thought the whole thing may never rise. But somehow, through sheer panic, determination, and an alarming amount of vanilla extract, it became something we are deeply proud to share with you.

Thank you for being here. Thank you for choosing live theatre over convenience, over streaming, over staying home. Your presence is part of the recipe.

We hope tonight leaves you feeling slightly fuller than when you arrived.

Bon appétit,

Alfred Kouris & Harry Stacey xx
